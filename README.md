# study-of-beaver-poop
A concise, professional — and slightly cheeky — field guide and research starter-kit for the study of beaver scat (scat, droppings, feces). Designed for educators, field biologists, and curious naturalists conducting observational or sample-based studies.

---

## Overview
This repository collects notes, field observations, photographic references, and methodological suggestions for identifying, describing, and responsibly sampling beaver feces in the field. The goal is to provide clear, reproducible guidance that supports education, ecological monitoring, and non-invasive biological research.

Key components:
- Field-friendly morphology and habitat cues
- Safety, ethics, and permitting guidance
- A curated image gallery for visual comparison
- Practical ideas for future analyses and projects

---

## Why it matters
Beaver feces are more than a gross-out curiosity — they are a window into ecosystem processes:
- Indicator of diet composition and seasonal resource use.
- Evidence of presence, territory boundaries, and latrine/feeding sites.
- Source material for non-invasive genetic, diet (metabarcoding), and pathogen monitoring.
- Educational tool for teaching about trophic interactions, riparian ecology, and field methods.

Properly documented scat data can support wetland restoration, population monitoring, and community science initiatives.

## Morphology notes
Observations below summarize typical traits and useful measurements for field recording. Variability is common; always pair morphology with contextual notes (location, date, weather, nearby trees/plants).

- General appearance: typically tubular or sausage-shaped pieces often with a fibrous texture. Can be found singly, in short piles, or as part of a latrine site.
- Color: ranges from dark brown to almost black when fresh; fades and lightens with exposure.
- Texture and contents: fibrous, often containing visible wood chips, bark fibers, and plant tissue. Aquatic plant fragments may be apparent in some seasons.
- Odor: variable; fresh scat has an earthy musky scent.
- Size (field estimate): larger than small rodent pellets but smaller than large ungulate droppings — record diameter and length where possible.
- Condition indicators: moisture content (fresh vs. dessicated), crumbly vs. compact, presence of insect activity or mold.

Recommended field metrics to record:
- GPS coordinates
- Date and time
- Freshness (fresh/stale/old)
- Approximate dimensions (length, diameter)
- Count (number of pieces, piles)
- Associated structures (dam, lodge, feeding sign)

## Habitat / Diet clues
Use scat as a context-rich data point to infer habitat use and seasonal diet patterns.

- Location within landscape:
  - Near dams, lodges, feeding platforms, or along shorelines indicates active territory and feeding routes.
  - Latrine sites (concentrated piles) often mark territorial boundaries and are useful for mapping presence.
- Diet inference:
  - Visible wood/bark fibers → woody browsing (willow, aspen, birch, poplar etc.).
  - Green plant fragments or aquatic vegetation → seasonal reliance on herbaceous/aquatic resources.
  - Presence of seeds, fruit fragments, or non-plant items should be noted for follow-up analysis.
- Seasonality:
  - Winter samples may show increased woody material as beavers rely on stored branches.
  - Summer samples may contain more aquatic and herbaceous material.
- Analytical follow-ups:
  - Macroscopic sorting and reference keys for common plant fragments.
  - Microscopic anatomy and pollen analysis.
  - DNA metabarcoding for robust dietary profiles.

## Ethics & Safety
Safety and ethics are paramount. Consider both human health and animal welfare.

Health precautions:
- Be aware of waterborne pathogens. "Beaver fever" (Giardia) is a real concern — avoid ingesting untreated water and avoid aerosolizing material.
- Wear nitrile gloves when handling scat; use disposable tools or sterilize instruments.
- Use a face covering if performing laboratory manipulations that could aerosolize material.
- Decontaminate surfaces and tools with appropriate disinfectants (e.g., 10% bleach solution followed by thorough rinsing) when required by your institutional protocol.

Field conduct and permitting:
- Obtain necessary permits before collecting samples on public or protected lands.
- Do not disturb beavers, their lodges, or dams. Avoid approaching or altering active structures.
- Minimize site impact: stick to established trails where possible and follow Leave No Trace principles.
- Respect local and Indigenous knowledge and land rights — consult communities where appropriate.

Data ethics:
- Anonymize sensitive location data when necessary to prevent poaching or habitat disturbance.
- Share methods and metadata to support reproducibility and proper interpretation.

If in doubt, consult your institutional biosafety officer (IBO), wildlife agency, or local extension service before sampling.
